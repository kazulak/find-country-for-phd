Ecosystem Realities: Employee vs. Student Models

💼

The Employee Model

Common in the Netherlands, Denmark, Sweden, Switzerland, and STEM fields in Germany. You are hired as a junior researcher on a collective employment agreement. You get a salary, pension contributions, paid sick leave, and unemployment insurance. Work is highly regulated, but expectations and teaching duties are standard parts of the job.

🎓

The Student model

Standard in the UK, Italy, and some scholarship tracks in France and Germany. You are registered as a postgraduate student. Your income is a tax-free stipend. While you pay little to no taxes, you do not build up pension savings, and you lack standard employee rights and union protections. Programs are often faster (3 to 3.5 years).

Ecosystem Terms Explained

What is E13 / TV-L?

In Germany, public employee pay scales are regulated by the TV-L agreement. Grade E13 is the standard grade for university graduates. You might be offered a 50%, 65%, or 100% position. STEM is usually 75-100%, whereas Humanities are often 50-65%.

PhD as a Job vs Student

Salaried employees pay income tax and social security, but build up valuable state and occupational pension credits and enjoy full parental leave rights. Stipend holders pay no tax but acquire no pension history or national insurance credits.

Search Year / Zoekjaar

Several European countries provide stay-back visas (from 6 to 18 months) allowing non-EU students to seek professional employment without standard corporate sponsorship hurdles. Sweden offers a path directly to permanent residency after 4 years of PhD research.
